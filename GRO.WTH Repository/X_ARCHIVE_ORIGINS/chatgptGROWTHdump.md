Skills and Advancement

Mechanically and narratively, Harvests are milestones or time-skips that allow characters to develop in ways beyond what immediate gameplay covers

The GM (often with help from The Terminal) uses these narratives to determine appropriate Harvest rewards for each character

Wealth System
GROWTH forgoes meticulous coin-counting in favor of an abstract Wealth Level system that measures a character’s overall financial resources and lifestyle. Wealth Levels range from 1 (Destitute) to 10 (Magnate), each corresponding to a general tier of wealth and influence
Google Drive
Google Drive
. Instead of tracking every gold piece, a character’s Wealth Level gives a broad sense of what they can afford and how comfortably they live:
Level 1 – Destitute: You have virtually nothing. Meeting basic needs like food and shelter is a daily struggle. You scavenge or beg and own only the clothes on your back (if that). Equipment is improvised or hand-me-down. Example: a vagabond with a stick and a bindle
Google Drive
.
Level 3 – Poor: You can cover basic living expenses and have a small bit of disposable income, but life is modest. You might have a few pieces of low-quality gear and often have to save up or take odd jobs for anything extra
Google Drive
.
Level 5 – Modest (Middle-Class): A stable, unremarkable living. You can afford standard equipment, occasional small luxuries, and have some savings. Perhaps you rent a decent home or have a reliable trade income
Google Drive
.
Level 7 – Wealthy: A life of comfort and luxury. You likely own property, wear fine clothes, and can acquire high-quality gear readily. You have influence in economic circles and can fund significant projects or favors
Google Drive
.
Level 10 – Magnate: Nearly unlimited financial power. You control vast resources (massive corporations, kingdoms, or vaults of treasure). You can buy almost anything money can buy and even sway markets or nations with your wealth
Google Drive
. At this level, wealth itself becomes a form of power and plot device.
In practical terms, Wealth Level determines what purchases you can make without hassle. The rules break down purchases by comparison to your Wealth:
If an item’s value is below your Wealth Level, you can buy it freely as long as it’s reasonable to find
Google Drive
. For instance, a Wealth 5 (Comfortable) character can buy common gear of value 4 or less without worrying – it’s like an average person buying groceries. The assumption is these expenses are trivial to you. (The GM can still veto absurd bulk buys, see below.)
If an item’s value equals your Wealth Level, you can afford it, but it’s a noticeable expense. Mechanically, you must mark off one “Wealth Check”
Google Drive
. Each Wealth Level is imagined to have three checks (think of these as three significant purchases you can make at that level before your finances dip)
Google Drive
. Buying something equal to your level uses one check – e.g., a Level 6 character (Affluent) buying a Level 6 item (like an expensive horse or fancy plasma rifle) would tick one of their three checks. They still remain Wealth 6 until they’ve spent all three checks. After a refresh period or major income (GM’s discretion), some checks could be restored, but generally they don’t reset easily without in-game earnings
Google Drive
.
If an item’s value is one level above your Wealth, you can scrape together the money, but it immediately drops your Wealth by one level
Google Drive
. Essentially, you stretched your finances thin or went into debt. For example, if that Wealth 6 character really wants a Level 7 item (say a small ship), they can buy it but their Wealth Level becomes 5 afterwards (and no checks left at 6 because they aren’t 6 anymore). This represents spending beyond your means – you liquidated assets or took a loan, etc.
If an item is more than one level above your Wealth, you simply can’t afford it; it’s out of reach without some extraordinary action or plot (like finding a patron or a treasure)
Google Drive
. A Level 4 character can’t directly purchase a Level 7 artifact through normal means, for example.
This system is balanced by the Wealth Checks mentioned. Every full Wealth Level has three checks (think of them like three chances to spend at that level)
Google Drive
. When you make a purchase equal to your level and use up the last (third) check, your Wealth Level decreases by one (you’ve effectively spent enough to drop a category of wealth)
Google Drive
. This drop might mean selling property, depleting savings, etc., in narrative terms. It prevents a character from infinitely buying level-equal items. Conversely, significant windfalls can restore checks or even raise Wealth Level: if you loot a dragon’s hoard or secure a lucrative trade deal, the GM might grant you more checks back or bump you up a Wealth Level
Google Drive
. The system is meant to be guideline-based and flexible. The rulebook explicitly says the GM has discretion to avoid abuse. For instance, if a player tries to exploit “I can buy anything below my level freely” by purchasing 100 swords of level 4 when they are Wealth 5, the GM can step in and limit bulk purchases

. The idea is to capture the spirit of a character’s wealth without micromanaging coins. If you’re Wealthy, you act wealthy; if you’re Destitute, you act poor, and the rules support that narratively. Example Scenarios:

If that knight wanted a truly rare artifact valued at 7, he can’t outright buy it because it exceeds his means; he’d need to find another way (perhaps do a quest for it, or find a sponsor)

.

The system ensures that financial decisions stay meaningful: players can’t just buy their way out of every problem, but neither do they have to count coppers for every inn stay.
